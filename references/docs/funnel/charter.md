# charter.md

Type: Operating constraints
Question: Who are we and how do we work?
Path: `osis/charter.md`

Instantiated only when multiple products genuinely share one parent set of beliefs and constraints. Repo topology does not create a charter by itself.

---

## Section scaffolds

Reasoning principles the agent runs silently before engaging each section. Not surfaced to the user. Principles are truths, not criteria.

### Mission

Principles:
- Mission names the change in the world the org exists to cause, not the product it sells.
- Mission is one sentence a stranger can repeat after one read.
- Mission is stable across pivots in product, market, and team composition.
- Mission is testable: an outsider can judge whether a given decision advances it.
- Purpose starts outside the organization; a mission that begins with "we" has not yet named the change it causes.

### Values

Principles:
- Values are the beliefs the org would hold even at a cost to growth, revenue, or speed.
- Values discriminate; they must plausibly exclude some people, customers, or tactics.
- Values describe behavior under pressure, not aspiration under ideal conditions.
- Values are lived in the thousand small decisions (hiring, writing, shipping), not declared on a wall.
- Generic virtues (integrity, excellence, respect) are table stakes, not identity.

⚠️ tension: Collins treats values as discovered and permanent; Lencioni and current founder writing warn that most stated values are aspirational theater unless they actively exclude.

### Non-Negotiables

Principles:
- A non-negotiable is a line the org refuses to cross even when crossing it would win.
- Non-negotiables are specified as refusals, not preferences; they name the behavior, customer, or shortcut declined.
- A non-negotiable that has never cost the org anything is not yet proven to be one.
- Non-negotiables are few; each additional one weakens the set.

### Decision Principles

Principles:
- Decisions are ranked by reversibility: irreversible choices get disproportionate deliberation, reversible ones get speed.
- When values conflict, the order of precedence is declared in advance, not improvised in the moment.
- Decisions are written down before they are made; the writing is the thinking.
- The default tiebreaker is the user or the mission, named explicitly, not "balance."
- Disagreement is surfaced and resolved on the record; silent consensus is a failure mode.

⚠️ tension: Bezos-style "disagree and commit" assumes a clear decider; Basecamp-style calm assumes small teams with shared taste. The mechanism must match the org's size and decision volume.
