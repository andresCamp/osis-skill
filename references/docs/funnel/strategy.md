# strategy.md

Type: Allocation
Question: Where do we focus and how do we win?
Path: `osis/{version}/strategy.md`

**Cross-section invariant:** Every section encodes the trade-off being accepted. Strategy is the allocation of scarce attention; a section that claims everything has not yet chosen anything.

---

## Section scaffolds

Reasoning principles the agent runs silently before engaging each section. Not surfaced to the user. Principles are truths, not criteria.

### Target

Principles:
- The target is who the product is obviously awesome for, not who could theoretically buy.
- Best-fit customers share a job-to-be-done and a shared dissatisfaction with existing alternatives.
- Targeting narrows reality; segments that include "everyone technical" are not targets.
- The sharper the target, the faster the feedback loop that validates or kills the thesis.

⚠️ tension: canonical frameworks (Moore, ODI) treat targeting as market math; greenfield (Dunford) treats it as a positioning choice anchored in competitive alternatives.

### Wedge

Principles:
- A wedge is the narrowest job where the product wins unambiguously against the nearest alternative.
- Wedges are entry points, not ceilings; worth is judged by what they open, not what they capture today.
- The competitive alternative to a wedge is usually "do nothing" or a manual workaround, not a named competitor.
- A wedge that requires behavior change and a new workflow at once is two wedges and will fail as one.

### Why Us

Principles:
- Differentiation is the set of features and context only this team can credibly deliver right now.
- Positioning defines what makes the product different and better for a specific customer against specific alternatives.
- Moats are built during the window before serious competition arrives; strategy decides how that window is spent.
- Founder-market fit is a source of advantage when the builder's lived context compresses the learning loop.
- Being first is not an advantage; being last to have to defend a position is.

### GTM / Distribution

Principles:
- The product is the primary vehicle of acquisition, activation, and retention when the buyer can self-serve the value.
- GTM motion follows price and complexity: self-serve for low-friction value, sales-assisted when the buyer needs context translated.
- Distribution is a design constraint of the product, not a layer added after launch.
- Activation happens when the user experiences the promised outcome, not when they sign up.
- Products that sell themselves through use outcompete products that require selling; distribution built into the product beats distribution bolted on.

### Success Criteria

Principles:
- A north star metric is the single measure whose increase most accelerates the business flywheel.
- Metrics persist as long as the strategy holds; a new strategy requires a new metric.
- Leading indicators earn their place by causally preceding the north star, not by being easy to move.
- Targets without timeframes are aspirations; timeframes without targets are calendars.
- Retention and repeat use are the only evidence that value was delivered; growth without them is borrowed.

⚠️ tension: canonical OKRs treat metrics as commitments; greenfield (Cutler) treats them as beliefs under test.

### Non-Goals

Principles:
- Every unspoken non-goal is a future distraction that will arrive dressed as opportunity.
- Scope expansion dilutes the core value proposition faster than under-investment starves it.
- Declining adjacent customers, features, and channels is the operational form of focus.
- A strategy without non-goals has not yet made a choice.

### Risks

Principles:
- The largest risk is the one the team has stopped examining because it feels settled.
- Running out of cash and lack of market need together account for most strategic death; both are detectable early if watched.
- A risk without a named owner and a trigger condition is not a risk, it is a worry.
- A premortem surfaces failure modes while they are still cheap; the cost of the exercise is always less than the cost of the failure.
- Mitigation is asymmetric: accept small reversible losses to avoid large irreversible ones, and structure bets so upside is unbounded and downside is bounded.
